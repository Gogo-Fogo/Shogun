# ART-007: Violence and Injury Policy

**Summary:** Production rules for how dark, bloody, and physically brutal `Shogun` should look while staying store-safe, stylistically coherent, and commercially viable for a collectible mobile RPG.

## Purpose

Use this note when deciding:

- how much blood, bruising, and visible injury is acceptable
- what kind of violent finishers are safe versus risky
- how much brutality a samurai-and-demons setting should imply versus show
- whether human and non-human enemies can be handled differently
- how to preserve dark-fantasy intensity without turning the game into gore-first content

## Executive rule

`Shogun` should aim for:

- **stylized violence**
- **battle consequence**
- **harsh but controlled injury**
- **dark-fantasy brutality implied more often than anatomically shown**

It should avoid:

- gore-forward spectacle
- dismemberment as a routine reward beat
- realistic decapitation
- prolonged graphic suffering
- sexualized injury or humiliation

The target is:

- darker than a bloodless fantasy RPG
- safer and more restrained than a gore-driven action game

## Why this line exists

This project wants:

- a world that feels dangerous and lethal
- demons, yokai, martial arts, and samurai themes to feel serious
- store survivability on Apple and Google Play
- a roster that stays collectible rather than becoming exploitation-heavy

That means violence should function as:

- a tone-setting tool
- a consequence signal
- a worldbuilding cue
- a contrast enhancer in damage states or aftermath art

It should **not** become the product’s main spectacle.

## Acceptable violence zone

### Good targets

- bruises
- cuts
- bloodstains
- bloody cloth or armor damage
- blood on weapons after combat
- restrained hit blood effects
- battle-worn portraits or damage states
- cursed ash, smoke, ink, or spirit residue for supernatural kills

### Good emotional tones

- dangerous
- painful
- grim
- desperate
- enraged
- exhausted
- battle-tested

### Good contexts

- duel aftermath
- cursed battlefields
- execution reputation in lore
- demon slaying with supernatural residue
- ritual combat
- corruption marks or blood moon atmosphere

## Red-line zone

Avoid:

- exposed organs
- graphic neck wounds
- heads visibly flying off
- realistic decapitations
- routine dismemberment
- torture imagery
- prolonged suffering framed for shock value
- violent fetish or humiliation logic

If the main thing a viewer remembers is "that was gruesome," the design has likely gone too far for this game.

## Blood and bruise rule

Blood and bruising are acceptable when they read as:

- battle wear
- consequence
- fatigue
- danger
- aftermath

They should not read as:

- gore showcase
- splatter reward
- repeated shock punctuation

### Good cues

- a bloody lip
- cut cheek or brow
- darkened bruise around an arm, shoulder, or leg
- blood on sleeves, kimono hems, or bandages
- cracked armor with a little blood at the edge
- limited slash trails or impact blood

### Bad cues

- blood jets
- pools of realistic gore as routine combat language
- injury framing that lingers on anatomy
- damage art whose main point is bodily suffering rather than character contrast

## Decapitation and dismemberment rule

### Humans

Do **not** show on-screen decapitations.

If a character is an executioner, head-taker, or feared duelist, communicate that through:

- lore
- reputation
- aftermath implication
- silhouettes
- dialogue
- bloodied blades
- abrupt black-frame or slash-cut storytelling

### Demons and yokai

Do not use realistic beheading as a routine gameplay beat.

Safer substitutes:

- disintegration into ash, smoke, embers, petals, or shadow
- horn, mask, or appendage breakage
- silhouette severing
- cursed-body collapse
- spirit-body unraveling

The fantasy can still feel deadly without showing a severed head rolling across the screen.

## Animation rule

Combat animation should sell:

- force
- lethality
- skill
- consequence

It should avoid:

- gore as payoff
- visible body-part detachment
- excessive blood volume
- prolonged impact shots designed to revel in injury

### Good violence animation tools

- strong anticipation and follow-through
- sharp impact silhouettes
- slash arcs
- hit-stop
- dust, ash, sparks, petals, ink, smoke, talisman bursts
- brief restrained blood accents
- collapse, stagger, recoil, and posture change

### Not recommended

- explicit dismemberment
- "fatality" style finishers
- head-removal animations
- gore-driven boss kills

## Interaction with fanservice

Violence must not merge with the game’s fanservice lane.

Avoid:

- sexualized crying or injury
- "damaged" art that reads as both erotic and physically degrading
- boob/ass-focused motion plus visible suffering
- battle damage framed like softcore defeat content

The goal is:

- "she looks like she survived a hard battle"

Not:

- "this is eroticized harm"

## Roster-balance rule

Different character types can express violence differently:

- noble or court characters: cleaner injury, dignified aftermath
- ronin or antiheroes: bloodier blades, harsher silhouettes, rougher posture
- yokai: strange non-human damage, spectral breakup, supernatural residue
- corrupted champions: cursed wounds, black blood, ritual damage motifs

This keeps the roster varied without pushing every lane toward gore.

## Implementation rule for Shogun

Use violence differently by asset lane.

### Battle assets

- blood kept light and readable
- no explicit severing
- brutality sold through timing and silhouette

### Collection assets

- can show more aftermath and atmosphere
- can use bloodstains, torn clothing, cracked armor, and strained expressions
- still avoid gore-forward imagery

### Damage art

- use bruising, cuts, torn fabric, and bloodstains
- preserve character dignity and identity
- keep it battle-worn, not mutilation-forward

## Dark-fantasy design takeaway

The right lesson for `Shogun` is:

- be ruthless
- not graphic

Samurai and demon themes do **not** require explicit beheading to feel serious. Often the stronger style choice is to imply lethal violence rather than diagram it.

## Platform and rating constraint

This policy exists partly because store policy and ratings matter.

Current constraints:

- Apple allows violence but treats prolonged graphic or sadistic realistic violence as a much riskier rating category
- Google Play allows fictional violence but not gratuitous gore-forward content
- ESRB treats blood and gore plus intense violence as mature territory

That means `Shogun` should stay in a dark, violent, but non-gory lane rather than trying to make brutality the product’s headline spectacle.

## Decision rule

When unsure, ask:

1. is this showing consequence, or chasing shock?
2. does this make the world feel harsher, or just gorier?
3. would this still be easy to defend in an App Store or Google Play review?
4. does this preserve collectible character appeal, or push the game toward exploitation?

If those answers are weak, scale it back.
